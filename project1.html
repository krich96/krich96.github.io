Kitchen Appetite for iOS

ROLE: RESEARCH, UX/UI DESIGN, PRODUCT MANAGEMENT - 2020

Kitchen Appetite is a Kickstarter-funded app that helps home cooks discover simple meals they can make with foods they have on hand. Perfect for quarantine! I was brought on to revamp the UX and UI midway through it's development.

The Problem

The original vision for Kitchen Appetite was an effortless recipe-finding experience where a user could 'scan' ingredients they had on hand using their phone camera (i.e. Shazam for your fridge).Although I was brought on for interface design, I soon found that there were deeper issues with the core value proposition of the app and understanding of the user.Part of this project was discovering our eventual problem statement: people often don't know what to make with the food the have.

An overseas development team built a functional prototype from a set of lo-fidelity wireframes (from another designer). Here is the 'before' app:

I tested the prototype and found some key problem areas:

  • 1. The image-recognition API was error-prone.

    Current technology could not achieve the effortless food-scanning experience envisioned by the founder. For this technology to be useful (and not just a gimmick), it would have to be as easy and accurate as typing into a search bar.

  • 2. Searching recipes by ingredients had limitations.

    Users could save unlimited ingredients, but could only search recipes by 6 ingredients. This led to an awkward 2-step process to search. I also questioned the utility of saving a long list of ingredients (food in the fridge changes quickly).

  • 3. The solution lacked a problem

    Even if this product existed as envisioned, it was not clear what kind of cook the product was for or the pain point that would make them open up the app to scan ingredients.

After discussing my concerns with the founder, we agreed to do some need-finding research. We also landed on a hypothesis for a problem statment: People often have perishable food items that they don't know what to do with. My research aimed to validate this hypothesis.

Research

My research covered three areas:

  1. 1. User interviews
  2. 2. Survey
  3. 3. Technology evaluation (testing other image recognition APIs)
  4. 4. Competitive Analysis of existing recipe apps

I then created a primary persona for Kitchen Appetite using the results of the user research.

USER INTERVIEWS & SURVEY

I interviewed 5 individuals between the ages of 26 and 48. All made over half of their meals, although many were packaged. 2 of 5 planned weekly meals in advance, with many homecooked meals being 'in the rotation' of recipes. 4 of 5 reported that they improvised in the kitchen, and 2/5 said they would often be unsure how they would prepare a food item they had. 5/5 said searching by ingredient would be helpful, but were skeptical of image recognition being helpful.

To discover more about the user persona I developed a survey to give to Kitchen Appetite backers (from Kickstarter) and social media communities. One key assumption was that not knowing what to cook is a big problem. In our sample of 22 survey respondants. This assumption got strong support. When asked to select what their biggest barrier to cooking was, 47% chose “Not enough time” but second to that, 24% marked “not knowing what to cook."

Exploratory questions helped us understand the needs, goals, and habits of our primary cook persona. They were most likely working people, cooking for their family. The goal of making a meal everyone would like was more pressing than the goal of making something interesting or amazing.

TECHNOLOGY ASSESSMENT

The Yummly, Google Vision, and Amazon Rekognition were some of the alternative food image recognition APIs to ours (Calorie Mama). My testing of all of these APIs found that none were accurate enough with food recognition to compete with a search bar. Even accurately identified foods were often tagged according to a general category (i.e., cheese but not blue cheese), which can have big implications for recipes.

The founder was excited to pursue this technology, and wanted to include it in the app beta. However we agreed it would be a secondary way to add ingredients and not the primary means.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I evaluated several different apps in terms of their user experience and product focus. Most had a range of recipe levels (easy to complex). Several helped you build a shopping list and purchase groceries. Yummly was the only app that offered flow of adding a few ingredients to find a recipe (e.g. improvising with whats on hand). The recipe matches for this flow were not always so simple. When considering the user interviews I had completed, it was clear that if cooks were in the mode of improvising a meal, recipe matches that were simple needed to prioritized (so little to no shopping was required).

PERSONA CREATION & PRODUCT DEFINITION

The new pitch: Kitchen Appetite helps you improvise a simple meal with foods you have on hand.

The research validated the hypothesis that people get stuck knowing what to make. Solving this problem resonated with the founder, even though image recognition wouldn't be the main way we solved it. We loved the idea of helping someone like Jess improvise a simple delicious meal (only one app we saw did that). The recipe ideas would be simple rather than aspirational, with the simplest shown first. This refocusing inspired the founder to start building branded 'ingredient guides' that help users make simple meals from a basic ingredient.

User Flows

With our shiny new persona, I then created a list of tasks, and began to create journey maps of different scenarios.

Wireframes, Prototype, and Testing

I created a set of wireframes and put them into a lo-fidelity prototype.

I brought this wireframe to the development team. They found no real problems except one. The image scanner needed a button in order to limit the number of API calls to the image recognition service we were using. That was simple enough to add. User testing sessions were conducted with 5 individuals. 5/5 were able to add an ingredient using all 3 methods. The participants also appreciated the narrow focus of the app and emphasis on simple recipes only.

Visual Design and Hi-fidelity Mockups

These were the three words Ben felt captured the Kitchen Appetite brand. He wanted the vibe to touch on nostalgia, family in the kitchen, and something playful but mature. The logo inspiration was colored alphabet fridge magnets. The logo had many wonderful colors to play with.

I created a mood board to express the visual identity and color palette. I took the green, brown, and blue from the logo and a lighter blue for a simple palette. I decided that ingredient photography could use simple backgrounds and recipe photography would be more complex, showing process (stirring) or plating on casual-looking tables or counters. I felt we could also use custom illustration for Kitchen Appetite's ingredient guides (in process), and thought a simple, abstract illustration style would work well with the brand.
